‘Here we are’ – Community-Based Sociotherapy in Byumba, Rwanda

‘Here we are’ Community-Based Sociotherapy in Byumba, Rwanda Three years ago in September 2005 a sociotherapy program started in the former Byumba province in the North of Rwanda. Sociotherapy is a method which enables people to come in small groups together, to reconnect and rebuild their personal lives and their community. This book is written mainly by sociotherapists themselves. It tells about their perception of life and what they were able to give to their community through sociotherapy. Composed by Annemiek Richters, Susan van Brakel en Cora Dekker

Rwanda’s Mediation of Memorialization through Community-Based Sociotherapy

Rwanda’s Mediation of Memorialization through Community-Based Sociotherapy

Written by Annemiek Richters, Grace Kagoyire, Theophile Sewimfura & Angela Jansen

Published: JUL 8 2014

(This article is part of an E-IR series marking the twentieth commemoration of the Rwandan Genocide.)

Introduction and Context

This year’s commemoration period in Rwanda marked the 20th anniversary of the Rwandan genocide against the Tutsi. Its motto was threefold: remember – unite – renew. The intention of Rwanda’s annual memorialization is to acknowledge the country’s violent history, to heal the wounds, to reduce societal tensions, and, in so doing, to prevent the recurrence of collective violence in the future.

During the genocide, about one million Rwandans were murdered. The identities of ‘victim’ and ‘perpetrator’ can not be reserved exclusively for specific groups, as many Hutus were also persecuted, and some Hutus risked their lives to save Tutsis.

The genocide started on the $7^{th}$ of April $1994$ and ended on the $4^{th}$ of July $1994$. The authors explore the extent to which Rwandans have been liberated from genocide-related memories that have the potential to be self-destructive, with reference to the impact of Community-Based Sociotherapy. The authors were particularly interested in the impact sociotherapy has had on its graduates in terms of the healing and reconciliation objectives of the commemoration.

Community-Based Sociotherapy in Rwanda

Community-based sociotherapy was introduced in the north of Rwanda in $2005$. It aims to assist people in dealing with the negative impacts of the war and genocide on their daily lives and psychosocial wellbeing, and to contribute to sustainable processes of reconciliation within communities.

The intervention focuses on the reconstruction of dignity, respect, trust, and safety among Rwandans.

Approach and Phases:

  • Group Size and Duration: Conducted in small groups of $10$ to $15$ people, meeting weekly for approximately three hours over a period of fifteen weeks.
  • Location: Takes place in a safe location in the group members’ living environment (school, church, under a tree, etc.).
  • Phases: Two trained facilitators guide the group through the sociotherapy phases of safety, trust, care, respect, new rules, and memories.
  • Key Principles (Applied throughout): Interest, equality, democracy, participation, responsibility, learning-by-doing, and the here-and-now.
  • Focus on Care: The phase of ‘care’ is where healing of social and individual distress starts to take effect, encouraging participants to solve current problems and think constructively about the future.
  • Memory Handling: Confrontation with painful memories is not encouraged at the start. The initial focus is on actual daily life problems (poverty, family conflicts, drug abuse). Painful memories are usually shared towards the end, once trust is established.

Distinctive Characteristics:

  • Participants work in small groups sitting in a circle.
  • Groups are formed of people from the same neighborhood who continue to meet after completion.
  • Inclusion is regardless of education, gender, age, religion, or historical identity.
  • Meetings take place in the living environment, not in training centers.
  • Facilitation is conducted by men and women who usually live in the same neighborhood.
  • Kinyarwanda is the primary language spoken.
  • No meals or money are provided; participation is driven by a sincere wish to heal.

The Official Mourning Week in Rwanda

The post-genocide Rwandan government developed a policy of ‘national unity and reconciliation’. Genocide memorials and commemorations are the most visible forms of implementation.

Commemoration Activities:

  • Ceremonies are held in stadiums, offices, and memorials across the country.
  • Activities include speeches, testimonies, prayers, reburial of human remains, candlelit vigils, and passing nights around the fire.
  • Messages of ‘remember’, ‘never forget’, and the credo ‘never again’ are omnipresent.
  • Citizens are expected to converge at venues to pay homage to victims, attend conferences, and comfort survivors.

Topics of Afternoon Lectures (Village Level):

  • The importance of commemoration.
  • The history of the genocide.
  • Identification of trauma symptoms and how to support traumatized people.
  • The fight against genocide and genocidal ideology.
  • Genocide’s consequences for the younger generation and the expected contribution of youth to fight against future genocide.
  • The prevention of and fight against negligence regarding the genocide.
  • Care for safety inside and outside the country.
  • Strategies to safeguard justice.
  • How to take care of one’s mental health.
  • How to contribute to economic growth.
  • Good governance.

Approach to Data Collection

The general themes guiding the data collection were: the meaning of commemoration, the diverse ways it is experienced, and the possible difference Community-Based Sociotherapy has made.

The authors spoke with a small number of $13$ sociotherapy graduates in the north and east of Rwanda over the course of the mourning week. The sample included:

  • $3$ genocide survivors ($2$ males, $1$ female)
  • $2$ ex-prisoners convicted of genocide crimes ($1$ male, $1$ female)
  • $1$ spouse of an ex-prisoner (female)
  • $3$ children of an ex-prisoner ($2$ males, $1$ female)
  • $1$ child of a genocide survivor (female)
  • $3$ returnees (males)

(Note: The authors emphasize that the outcomes are a snapshot and not representative of all different perceptions among the Rwandan population.)

Meaning Attribution and Living Through the Mourning Week

All respondents attributed great value to the mourning week, emphasizing that remembering is important in order not to forget.

Ex-Prisoners: Recalled that in prison, they did not like to hear songs or watch TV news about the genocide during commemoration because it worsened feelings of guilt and shame. They value the week as a special occasion to regret their deeds and ask for forgiveness.

Genocide Survivors: Batistuta noted that ceremonies re-open the wounds but act as a mirror for everybody, enabling them to deal with the past. Some suffered severely, while others like Chamima and Constance felt strong and completely safe this year.

Ex-Prisoner Frida: Used neighbourhood meetings to sit with survivors whom she usually fears, as a way to show she is a changed person and to take care of those crying.

The Contribution of Sociotherapy to Healing and Reconciliation

Most respondents felt that sociotherapy had made a difference in their experience of the mourning period compared to before.

  • Reduced Aggression (Batistuta): Before sociotherapy, Batistuta was self-destructive and once tried to attack an ex-prisoner. Now, he has reconciled with that ex-prisoner.
  • Increased Safety (Georges): Georges previously suffered from nightmares and avoided commemoration. Sociotherapy helped him understand why survivors acted aggressively, and a feeling of safety emerged within the group, which improved his public participation.
  • Focus on Others (Bernard): Learned to share experiences and take care of others: "Instead of judging other people, I can now take care of them, because everyone... is still facing the genocide consequences."
  • Elimination of Panic Attacks: Female genocide survivors who had experienced rape often suffered severely from panic attacks during the mourning week. For all women interviewed, this is no longer the case.

Conclusion

The stories illustrate that sociotherapy is an approach that can prepare and help people live through the mourning week in a way that invigorates them, instead of re-traumatizing and socially isolating them.

The newly developed coping strategies help participants live through the commemoration period in a way that is experienced, at least to some extent, as healing and reconciliatory. Memories no longer generate self-destructive or harmful actions.

  • Survivors have found the strength to go beyond their own pain and now reach out to others and care for them.
  • Former perpetrators have learned to cope with shame and guilt and have also started to reach out to those they harmed, starting a process of reconciliation.
  • The empathy and care for one another, as developed in sociotherapy, has substantially increased support for genocide survivors and participation in memorial activities in neighborhoods where the practice is common.

INFOGRAPHIC: Mvura Nkuvure Milestones for Round One in Nyabihu district

As part of the implementation process of the Tujyane Project (Sustainable Peacebuilding through Intergenerational Healing) in the Nyabihu district, the Mvura Nkuvure round one was concluded after eight months of consecutive weekly sessions of Mvura Nkuvure in three sectors of Nyabihu district: Bigogwe, Mukamira, and Jenda sectors, since 2020. 240 participants were selected to attend the sessions and half of them, after graduation, started already income-generating activities and others are planning to start soon.
